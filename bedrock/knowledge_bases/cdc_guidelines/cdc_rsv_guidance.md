# CDC RSV Prevention and Response Guidance

*Source: CDC RSV Prevention (2024-25 Season), ACIP Recommendations for Nirsevimab, CDC RSV-NET Surveillance, MMWR Reports*

---

## RSV Overview for Public Health

Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) causes annual seasonal epidemics in the United States, typically from fall through spring. RSV is the leading cause of hospitalization in infants and a significant cause of respiratory illness in older adults.

### Season Timing (United States):
- **Typical onset**: October–November
- **Peak**: December–January (varies by region; Southern states often peak earlier)
- **Duration**: 4–5 months
- **Note**: Since the COVID-19 pandemic, RSV seasonality has shifted — monitor wastewater data for actual timing

## High-Risk Populations

### Highest Priority (severe disease/hospitalization risk):
1. **Infants <6 months of age** — highest hospitalization rate of any age group
2. **Premature infants** (<36 weeks gestational age)
3. **Children <2 years with chronic lung disease** (bronchopulmonary dysplasia)
4. **Children <2 years with hemodynamically significant congenital heart disease**
5. **Immunocompromised children** of any age
6. **Adults ≥65 years** — second leading cause of winter hospitalization after influenza
7. **Adults with chronic heart or lung disease** (COPD, CHF, asthma)
8. **Residents of long-term care facilities**
9. **Immunocompromised adults** (transplant recipients, active chemotherapy)

### Transmission Characteristics:
- Incubation period: 4–6 days
- Infectious period: 3–8 days (up to 4 weeks in immunocompromised)
- Transmission: Large droplets (close contact), fomites (surfaces — RSV survives hours)
- R₀: 2–3 (highly contagious in household/childcare settings)

## Immunoprophylaxis

### Nirsevimab (Beyfortus®) — Monoclonal Antibody for Infants
- **Indication**: All infants <8 months entering their first RSV season; children 8–19 months at increased risk entering second season
- **Dosing**: Single intramuscular injection (50 mg for <5 kg, 100 mg for ≥5 kg)
- **Timing**: Administer before or during RSV season (ideally October–November for most regions)
- **Supply**: Limited supply possible — prioritize highest-risk infants if constrained
- **Protection duration**: ~5 months from administration

### Palivizumab (Synagis®) — Legacy Option
- **Use**: Only when nirsevimab unavailable; limited to highest-risk infants
- **Dosing**: Monthly injection (15 mg/kg) throughout RSV season (up to 5 doses)
- **Cost**: ~$1,500–$3,000 per dose — significant burden for rural facilities

### RSV Vaccine for Older Adults (Arexvy®, Abrysvo®)
- **Indication**: Adults ≥60 years, shared clinical decision-making
- **Timing**: Single dose; administer before RSV season (September–October ideal)
- **Prioritize**: Long-term care facility residents, adults with chronic conditions

### RSV Vaccine for Pregnant Persons (Abrysvo®)
- **Indication**: Pregnant persons at 32–36 weeks gestation during September–January
- **Purpose**: Passive antibody transfer to protect infant in first months of life
- **Note**: Either maternal vaccination OR infant nirsevimab — not both routinely needed

## Hospital Capacity Planning for RSV Surge

### Pediatric Capacity Thresholds:

| Metric | Green (Normal) | Yellow (Concern) | Red (Crisis) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pediatric bed occupancy | <75% | 75–90% | >90% |
| PICU occupancy | <70% | 70–85% | >85% |
| Average pediatric ED wait time | <2 hours | 2–4 hours | >4 hours |
| Bronchiolitis admissions/week | Baseline | 1.5× baseline | 2× baseline |
| Respiratory therapy staff availability | Full | <85% | <70% |

### Surge Capacity Actions:

**Level 1 (Yellow — Concern):**
- Cancel elective pediatric procedures
- Activate additional respiratory therapy staff
- Ensure high-flow nasal cannula equipment available on all pediatric floors
- Pre-position suction equipment and bulb syringes
- Increase hand hygiene auditing

**Level 2 (Red — Crisis):**
- Open overflow pediatric unit (convert adult beds if needed)
- Request transfer agreements with regional children's hospitals
- Establish criteria for home oxygen therapy to free inpatient beds
- Deploy telehealth pediatric consultations for rural facilities
- Activate regional pediatric mutual aid

### Rural-Specific Considerations:
- Most rural counties lack pediatric-specific inpatient capacity
- Transfer time to nearest children's hospital may be 1–3 hours
- Establish transfer protocols BEFORE peak season
- Train rural ED staff on pediatric high-flow nasal cannula management
- Stock pediatric-sized equipment (smaller masks, appropriately sized NG tubes)

## Long-Term Care Facility Protocols

### Prevention:
- Vaccinate all eligible residents (RSV vaccine for ≥60 years)
- Vaccinate staff (influenza + COVID — no RSV vaccine for <60 staff yet)
- Implement respiratory illness screening at facility entry
- Limit visitors during high community RSV activity
- Cohort residents with respiratory symptoms

### Response to Suspected Outbreak (≥2 cases in 72 hours):
1. Implement droplet precautions for symptomatic residents
2. Restrict new admissions to affected unit
3. Notify state health department within 24 hours
4. Collect specimens for RSV confirmation (at least 3 cases)
5. Increase environmental cleaning (focus on handrails, door handles, call buttons)
6. Monitor all residents for symptoms twice daily (temperature + respiratory symptoms)
7. Restrict staff movement between affected and unaffected units

## Key Differences from Influenza Preparedness

| Factor | Influenza | RSV |
|---|---|---|
| Antiviral treatment | Yes (oseltamivir) | No — supportive care only |
| Vaccine (all ages) | Yes | Limited (infants + ≥60) |
| School closure impact | High | Lower (RSV peaks in younger children) |
| Hospitalization age peak | Elderly (≥65) | Infants (<1 year) AND elderly |
| Duration of illness | 5–7 days | 7–14 days (longer in infants) |
| Staff absenteeism impact | High | Moderate (adults milder) |
