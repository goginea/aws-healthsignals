# Inventory Management Strategies During Drug Shortages

_Source: ASHP Pharmacy Practice Model, FDA Best Practices for Drug Shortage Management, Healthcare Distribution Alliance (HDA)_

---

## Overview

Proactive inventory management is critical to maintaining continuity of patient care during drug shortages. This document provides best practices for health system pharmacies and rural health facilities to prepare for, respond to, and recover from pharmaceutical supply disruptions.

---

## Just-in-Time Inventory Adjustments

### Standard Operations vs. Shortage Mode

| Parameter             | Normal           | Shortage Active           | Rationale                                |
| --------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Days of supply target | 3-5 days         | 7-14 days                 | Buffer against supply interruptions      |
| Reorder point         | 2 days use       | 5-7 days use              | Earlier trigger for procurement action   |
| Source strategy       | Single preferred | Multi-source required     | Reduce dependency on single manufacturer |
| Safety stock          | Minimal          | 1-2 weeks additional      | Absorb delivery variability              |
| Dispensing quantity   | Standard         | Conservative (acute need) | Extend available supply                  |

### Trigger Criteria for Shortage Mode

Transition from Normal to Shortage Mode when ANY of the following occur:

1. HealthSignals alert indicates NEW or WORSENING shortage for a formulary product
2. Primary wholesaler places product on allocation (reduced order fill)
3. Manufacturer communicates supply disruption with >2 week expected duration
4. Distributor backorder exceeds 5 business days with no estimated availability
5. FDA Drug Shortage site lists the product as "Currently in Shortage"

### Demand Analysis During Shortage

```
Current Average Daily Use:
  → Pull dispensing data for past 90 days
  → Identify top consuming units/services
  → Separate new starts from maintenance therapy

Projected Demand (next 30 days):
  → Scheduled procedures requiring this medication
  → Seasonal patterns (flu season, allergy season)
  → HealthSignals disease outbreak alerts that may increase demand
```

## Communication with Group Purchasing Organizations (GPOs)

### Role of GPOs During Shortages

- **Information hub** — Early warning of supply disruptions from manufacturers
- **Allocation coordinator** — Fair distribution among member facilities
- **Alternative sourcing** — Contract activation with secondary suppliers
- **Market intelligence** — Visibility into industry-wide availability

### Communication Best Practices

**Information to Provide to GPO:**

- Current inventory on hand (days of supply)
- Average daily utilization rate
- Patient population at risk if supply interrupted
- Acceptable alternative products already on contract

**Information to Request from GPO:**

- Manufacturer's estimated resolution timeline
- Alternative contracted products and their availability
- Allocation methodology and your facility's allocation percentage
- New contract activation timeline for alternative manufacturers

### Escalation Path

```
Level 1: Routine (no immediate patient care risk)
  → Monitor GPO portal updates weekly

Level 2: Supply at risk (< 2 weeks on hand)
  → Contact GPO account representative directly
  → Request allocation increase or emergency supply
  → Identify alternative contracted products

Level 3: Critical supply (< 3 days on hand)
  → Escalate to GPO senior leadership
  → Activate alternative sourcing immediately
  → Notify facility leadership of patient care risk
```

## Multi-Source Product Identification

### Diversification Strategy

**Pre-Shortage Preparation:**

- Identify at least 2 manufacturers for all critical medications
- Maintain active accounts with multiple wholesalers (primary + secondary)
- Evaluate 503B outsourcing facilities for compounded alternatives
- Document NDC numbers for all approved alternatives in pharmacy system

**Product Equivalence Verification:**

- Same active ingredient and strength
- Bioequivalence (AB-rated in FDA Orange Book for generics)
- Compatible route of administration
- Storage requirements compatible with your facility

### Source Priority Matrix

| Source                    | Lead Time | Use Case                             |
| ------------------------- | --------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Primary wholesaler        | 1-2 days  | Day-to-day supply                    |
| Secondary wholesaler      | 1-3 days  | When primary is allocated            |
| Direct from manufacturer  | 5-14 days | Large orders, commitment             |
| 503B outsourcing facility | 7-21 days | When no commercial product available |
| Other facilities (loan)   | Same day  | Critical patient need, bridge supply |

## Emergency Supply Coordination with Neighboring Facilities

### Mutual Aid Network

Establish relationships BEFORE shortages occur:

- Identify facilities within 50-mile radius (hospitals, health systems, long-term care)
- Exchange pharmacy director contact information and after-hours contacts
- Agree on communication protocols for supply requests
- Consider formal mutual aid agreements (MOUs)

### Communication Protocol During Active Shortage

```
Step 1: Assess internal situation
  → Current inventory (units and days of supply)
  → Minimum needed to maintain patient care

Step 2: Query network availability
  → Contact neighboring facilities via established channels
  → Specify: drug, strength, form, quantity needed, urgency

Step 3: Arrange transfer
  → Verify regulatory compliance for drug transfer in your state
  → Document chain of custody and storage conditions
  → Confirm product integrity (temperature, expiration, appearance)

Step 4: Document and report
  → Record transfer in inventory management system
  → Plan repayment when supply normalizes
```

### Rural-Specific Considerations

- Longer distances between facilities increase transfer logistics complexity
- State health department emergency pharmacy programs may provide assistance
- Critical Access Hospitals may qualify for emergency supply programs
- Telepharmacy networks can provide clinical support during decision-making

## Demand Forecasting During Disease Outbreaks

### Integration with HealthSignals Alerts

When HealthSignals generates a disease outbreak alert, assess potential medication demand:

| Outbreak Type | Medications Impacted                                         | Surge Multiplier |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------- |
| Influenza     | Antivirals, antibiotics (secondary infections), antipyretics | 2-5x baseline    |
| RSV           | Bronchodilators, corticosteroids (pediatric)                 | 2-3x baseline    |
| COVID-19      | Antivirals, corticosteroids, anticoagulants                  | 2-4x baseline    |

### Forecasting Methodology

```
Projected Demand = Baseline Daily Use × Surge Multiplier × Duration (days)
Safety Buffer = Projected Demand × 0.25
Total Procurement Target = Projected Demand + Safety Buffer - Current Inventory
```

**Timing Considerations:**

- HealthSignals provides 1-3 weeks advance warning before peak
- Use lead time to increase procurement before demand surge
- Order early — availability decreases as outbreak progresses nationwide

### Proactive Procurement Actions

| Timeframe       | Action                                                             |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Immediately     | Inventory audit of relevant therapeutic categories                 |
| Within 24 hours | Contact primary wholesaler to confirm allocation/availability      |
| Within 48 hours | Place supplemental orders for projected demand increase            |
| Within 1 week   | Activate multi-source procurement for at-risk medications          |
| Ongoing         | Monitor inventory daily, adjust orders based on actual utilization |
