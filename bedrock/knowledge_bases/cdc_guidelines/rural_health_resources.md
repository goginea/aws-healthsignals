# Rural Health Preparedness Resources

*Source: HRSA Federal Office of Rural Health Policy, NACCHO (National Association of County and City Health Officials), ASTHO (Association of State and Territorial Health Officials), National Rural Health Association, FEMA Community Preparedness Tools*

---

## Rural Health Definitions

### HRSA Rural Definitions:
- **Rural**: Any area NOT designated as an urbanized area (population ≥50,000) or urban cluster (population 2,500–49,999) by the US Census Bureau
- **Frontier**: Typically defined as ≤6 persons per square mile
- **Rural-Urban Continuum Codes (RUCC)**: Scale of 1-9 (codes 4-9 are considered rural)

### Key Statistics:
- ~60 million Americans live in rural areas (19% of US population)
- Rural counties occupy 97% of US land area
- 2,000+ local health departments serve predominantly rural populations
- Median rural LHD has <10 FTE staff
- 77% of rural counties are designated Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs)

## Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA) Considerations

### Impact on Preparedness:
- Fewer providers means LESS surge capacity
- Longer travel times to specialty care (average 30-60 minutes)
- Limited after-hours care options
- Provider burnout is baseline (even before a surge)

### Compensating Strategies:
1. **Telehealth triage**: Route non-emergency cases to telehealth to preserve in-person capacity
2. **Standing orders**: Enable pharmacists and nurses to initiate treatment protocols
3. **Regional collaboration**: Share staff through mutual aid agreements
4. **Community health workers**: Extend reach for surveillance and basic care
5. **Medical Reserve Corps (MRC)**: Pre-credentialed volunteers available for activation

## Mutual Aid Agreements

### What to Have in Place BEFORE Season:
- **Neighboring county MOUs**: Agree on conditions for staff sharing, equipment loans, patient transfers
- **Regional Healthcare Coalition membership**: Formal structure for resource sharing
- **EMAC (Emergency Management Assistance Compact)**: Interstate mutual aid framework
- **Hospital transfer agreements**: Pre-negotiate bed availability with regional hospitals

### Template Mutual Aid Components:
1. Triggering conditions (what activates the agreement)
2. Resources available (personnel types, equipment, supplies)
3. Command structure (who requests, who authorizes)
4. Liability and workers' comp coverage
5. Duration limits and renewal conditions
6. Communication protocols
7. Demobilization criteria

### Rural-Specific Challenges:
- Neighboring counties may be equally under-resourced
- Travel distances between facilities can be 50+ miles
- Communications infrastructure may be unreliable (limited cell coverage)
- Weather can disrupt mutual aid delivery (ice storms, flooding)

## Telehealth Surge Options

### Surge Telehealth Deployment (within 48 hours):
1. Activate existing telehealth platform for triage
2. Post telehealth number on health department voicemail and website
3. Partner with state telehealth network for provider capacity
4. Establish nurse triage line for symptomatic callers
5. Coordinate with local pharmacies for telepharmacy consultations

### Effective Telehealth Models for Rural Outbreaks:
- **Nurse triage hotline**: RN uses algorithm to sort callers into self-care, telehealth visit, or ED referral
- **Provider-on-call telehealth**: Local physician or NP sees low-acuity patients virtually, prescribes antivirals
- **Specialist consultation**: Rural ED connects to urban specialist via video for complex cases
- **Chronic disease management**: Continue routine care virtually to keep clinic capacity for acute illness

### Technology Considerations:
- Many rural patients lack broadband — telephone (audio-only) must be an option
- Hospital/clinic WiFi may be insufficient for video — test BEFORE activation
- Ensure HIPAA-compliant platform is already contracted and tested
- Train staff on telehealth workflows BEFORE they're needed

## Medical Reserve Corps (MRC) Activation

### What MRC Provides:
- Pre-credentialed volunteer healthcare professionals
- Trained community volunteers for non-clinical tasks
- Rapid activation for public health emergencies
- No cost to the requesting jurisdiction

### Activation Timeline:
1. **Day 1**: Request submitted to state MRC coordinator
2. **Day 2-3**: Volunteers contacted and availability confirmed
3. **Day 3-5**: Credentialing verification completed
4. **Day 5-7**: Volunteers deployed to requesting jurisdiction

### MRC Volunteer Capabilities:
- Licensed clinicians (physicians, nurses, pharmacists): Clinical care
- Non-clinical health professionals (health educators, epidemiologists): Surveillance and education
- General volunteers: Logistics, phone banking, supply distribution, data entry

### Best Practices for MRC Integration:
- Include MRC in regular exercises (not just activation during emergencies)
- Pre-assign roles and reporting locations
- Maintain current contact lists (update quarterly)
- Brief MRC on local protocols upon arrival

## State Epidemiologist Notification Thresholds

### When to Notify State Health Department:

**Immediate Notification (within 24 hours):**
- Unusual cluster of severe respiratory illness
- Death attributed to influenza in a person <18 years
- Novel influenza A virus detection
- Outbreak in healthcare facility or congregate setting
- Any condition on the state's mandatory reportable disease list

**Routine Notification (within 72 hours):**
- ILI rates exceeding local baseline by >50%
- Antiviral resistance detected
- School absenteeism exceeding 10% due to respiratory illness
- Healthcare facility staffing below 75% due to illness

**Informational (weekly report):**
- Local surveillance trends
- Vaccine uptake data
- HealthSignals alert status and predictions
- Mutual aid requests/activations

## Critical Access Hospital (CAH) Capacity Limits

### CAH Characteristics:
- Maximum 25 inpatient beds
- Average length of stay ≤96 hours (4 days)
- Located >35 miles from nearest hospital (or designated by state)
- 1,350+ CAHs across the US (many in rural counties)

### Typical CAH Surge Capacity:
- **Standard capacity**: 15-20 occupied beds
- **Surge (staffing allows)**: May flex to 25 beds maximum
- **Crisis capacity**: Convert observation/procedure areas to inpatient (~30 beds max)
- **Transfer threshold**: When >20 respiratory patients AND no ICU capability → begin transfers

### CAH Preparedness Checklist:
- Verify transfer agreements with referral hospital (confirm acceptance capacity)
- Stock respiratory support equipment (high-flow nasal cannula, BiPAP)
- Pre-identify patients for early discharge if beds needed
- Cross-train med-surg nurses for respiratory care protocols
- Establish criteria for "keep locally" vs "transfer" decision-making
- Coordinate ambulance/air medical availability for transfers

### HealthSignals Value for CAHs:
- 3-5 week advance warning allows time to:
  - Discharge stable patients early
  - Cancel elective procedures before beds are needed
  - Request additional staff from staffing agencies
  - Pre-position supplies from regional distribution centers
  - Coordinate with nearby CAHs for load-balancing

## Emergency Preparedness Funding Sources

### Available to Rural Health Departments:
- **PHEP (Public Health Emergency Preparedness)** — CDC cooperative agreement, annual
- **HPP (Hospital Preparedness Program)** — ASPR, via state to healthcare coalitions
- **HRSA Rural Health Grants** — Community-level preparedness activities
- **FEMA BRIC (Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities)** — mitigation funding
- **State/tribal emergency management funds** — vary by jurisdiction

### What Funding Can Support:
- Staff training and exercises
- Communication systems (alert platforms, satellite phones)
- Stockpile purchases (PPE, antivirals, testing supplies)
- Mutual aid agreement development
- Technology infrastructure (telehealth, surveillance systems)
