# FDA Drug Shortage Management Protocols

_Source: FDA Drug Shortage Guidance for Industry (2023), ASHP Guidelines on Managing Drug Product Shortages, FDA Manual of Policies and Procedures (CDER)_

---

## Overview

Drug shortages are a persistent challenge for healthcare systems. The FDA defines a drug shortage as "a period of time when the demand or projected demand for a medically necessary drug exceeds the supply." This document provides general guidance on managing shortages in alignment with FDA recommendations.

**Important:** This document provides frameworks only. All clinical decisions must be made by qualified healthcare professionals based on individual patient circumstances.

---

## Conservation Strategies

### Dose Optimization Principles

When a medication is in shortage, conservation through evidence-based dose optimization may extend available supply:

**General Approaches:**

- Review current utilization patterns for opportunities to reduce waste
- Evaluate automatic stop orders and default quantities
- Consider extended dosing intervals ONLY when supported by published evidence
- Audit preparation and dispensing practices for overfill waste

**Operational Conservation:**

- Reduce dispensing quantities to immediate-need amounts (avoid unit-level stockpiling)
- Review automated dispensing cabinet par levels downward
- Coordinate procedural area usage to minimize open-and-discard events
- Establish standardized concentrations to reduce compounding waste

**Duration-Based Strategies:**

- Evaluate therapy duration against current evidence (some therapies may be shortened)
- Implement antibiotic stewardship protocols for IV-to-oral step-down where applicable
- Consider dose-rounding for biologics and weight-based therapies to reduce vial waste

### Prioritization Framework

When supply is critically constrained, establish a prioritization protocol:

| Priority Level | Criteria                                                 | Action                                  |
| -------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Critical       | Life-threatening condition, no alternative exists        | Full allocation maintained              |
| High           | Serious condition, alternatives exist but are suboptimal | Reduced allocation, case-by-case review |
| Moderate       | Condition manageable with alternatives                   | Switch to alternative therapy           |
| Low            | Elective or preference-based use                         | Defer until shortage resolves           |

**Decision Authority:**

- Priority Level assignments require Pharmacy and Therapeutics (P&T) Committee review
- Individual patient exceptions require attending physician and clinical pharmacist agreement
- Document all prioritization decisions for quality assurance review

## Compounding Alternatives Framework

### General Principles

Compounding may provide an alternative supply source during shortages. This section outlines the regulatory framework — NOT specific compounding recipes or formulations.

**503A Pharmacies (Traditional Compounding):**

- Compound in response to individual patient prescriptions
- Must comply with state pharmacy practice acts
- Cannot compound drugs essentially copies of commercially available products (with shortage exceptions)
- FDA exercises enforcement discretion during declared shortages

**503B Outsourcing Facilities:**

- Register with FDA, subject to cGMP requirements
- May compound without individual prescriptions
- Products must appear on FDA's drug shortage list for certain exemptions

**Key Regulatory Considerations:**

- Verify the drug appears on the FDA Drug Shortage list before compounding
- Ensure Beyond-Use Dates are assigned per USP <797> or <795> standards
- Maintain documentation of shortage status at time of compounding decision

### When to Consider Compounding

```
Decision Tree:
1. Is the drug on the FDA shortage list?
   → NO: Do not compound a copy; seek commercial alternative
   → YES: Continue to step 2

2. Is a therapeutically equivalent commercial product available?
   → YES: Use the available product first
   → NO: Continue to step 3

3. Does your facility have compounding capability for this dosage form?
   → YES: Initiate compounding per applicable regulations
   → NO: Contact 503B outsourcing facilities or state compounding network
```

## Communication Protocols with Prescribers

### Notification Tiers

Communicate shortage information using a tiered approach based on clinical impact:

**Tier 1 — Informational (LOW priority shortages):**

- Channel: Pharmacy newsletter, intranet posting
- Timing: Within 1 week of detection
- Content: Drug name, current status, expected timeline, alternatives overview

**Tier 2 — Action Required (NEW or WORSENING, MEDIUM priority):**

- Channel: Email to affected service lines, EHR alert if available
- Timing: Within 48 hours of detection
- Content: Drug name, impact assessment, recommended alternatives, formulary changes

**Tier 3 — Urgent (NEW or WORSENING, HIGH priority, no alternatives):**

- Channel: Direct communication (page, phone) to affected prescribers
- Timing: Same day as detection
- Content: Immediate impact, patient-level guidance, escalation pathway

### Communication Content Standards

All prescriber communications during shortages should include:

1. **What is affected** — Drug name, strength(s), dosage form(s)
2. **Current status** — Available quantity, estimated duration
3. **Why** — Reason for shortage (manufacturing, raw material, demand increase)
4. **What to do** — Specific recommended actions or alternatives
5. **Who to contact** — Pharmacy point of contact for questions
6. **When to expect update** — Next communication timeline

## Patient Safety Considerations During Shortages

### Risk Assessment

**High-Risk Scenarios:**

- Narrow therapeutic index medications (anticoagulants, antiepileptics, immunosuppressants)
- Medications with no therapeutic alternative
- Pediatric or neonatal formulations with limited options
- Emergency/critical care medications

**Mitigation Strategies:**

- Enhanced monitoring when switching formulations or routes
- Patient and caregiver education about therapy changes
- Pharmacist clinical review of all forced therapeutic switches
- Error-prevention protocols (look-alike/sound-alike awareness for new stock)

### Medication Error Prevention

Shortages increase medication error risk. Prevention actions:

- Update clinical decision support systems when formulary changes occur
- Alert nursing staff about new product appearances (color, labeling changes)
- Add shortage-related alerts to automated dispensing cabinets
- Conduct independent double-checks for high-alert medication alternatives
- Brief relevant staff on new concentrations or dosage forms

### Patient Communication

When shortage affects individual patient care: notify patient of therapy change and rationale in plain language, explain expected outcomes, provide clear instructions for new regimen, offer pharmacy contact for questions, and document notification in medical record.

## Reporting and Monitoring Requirements

### FDA Reporting

**When to Report to FDA:**

- Supply disruption lasting longer than manufacturer's estimated timeline
- Shortage of a medically necessary drug not yet on FDA Drug Shortage site
- Quality concerns with shortage-related compounded products
- Significant clinical impacts from shortage (adverse events, treatment delays)

**Reporting Channels:**

- FDA Drug Shortage Staff: drugshortages@fda.hhs.gov
- MedWatch (for quality/safety concerns)
- ASHP Drug Shortage Resource Center

### Internal Monitoring Requirements

**Weekly Monitoring (during active shortage):**

- Current inventory levels vs. projected demand
- Patient impact metrics (delayed treatments, therapy switches)
- Supply chain signals (distributor allocation changes, new backorder notifications)

**Monthly Reporting (P&T Committee):**

- Active shortage summary by therapeutic category
- Clinical outcomes of forced therapeutic interchanges
- Conservation strategy effectiveness metrics

### Regulatory Compliance Checkpoints

| Activity                     | Frequency | Responsible Party           |
| ---------------------------- | --------- | --------------------------- |
| FDA shortage list review     | Weekly    | Drug Information Pharmacist |
| Compounding compliance audit | Monthly   | Quality Assurance           |
| Conservation protocol review | Quarterly | P&T Committee               |
| Shortage response drill      | Annually  | Pharmacy Leadership         |
